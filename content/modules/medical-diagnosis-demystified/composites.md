# Lived Experience Composites — Medical Diagnosis Demystified

## Composite 1: Confusion Upon Hearing Diagnosis Terminology

<!-- provenance: composite -->

When caregivers first hear the words "mild cognitive impairment" or "probable Alzheimer's disease," many describe a moment of disconnect. The term lands clinically precise to the clinician, but to the adult child sitting across the desk, it raises immediate, unanswered questions: Is this reversible? How long do we have? Can they still drive? Can they stay home alone? The diagnosis was supposed to answer what is wrong; instead, it opens a gap between the medical label and what it means Monday morning. Many caregivers report that the first response is not relief at having a name, but a second wave of confusion about what comes next. Some say they went home, looked up the terms online, and fell into worst-case scenarios. Others describe nodding in the appointment, feeling the weight of something they do not understand, and only much later asking clarifying questions when it felt too late to ask.

## Composite 2: Frustration with Diagnostic Workup Delays and Costs

<!-- provenance: composite -->

The diagnostic pathway often stretches longer than caregivers expect. Blood work, a cognitive test at the primary care office, then a referral to a neurologist, then a wait for the neurologist appointment, then imaging, sometimes a specialist review of the images—each step takes weeks. Insurance may decline imaging or request prior authorization, adding delay. By the time a family receives a diagnosis, months may have passed. Caregivers often express not anger at the clinician, but frustration at the system's slowness: they came wanting clarity to make decisions (whether to step back from work, move closer to family, explore care options), and instead they got more waiting. The costs compound the friction. Insurance coverage for biomarker blood tests remains inconsistent; MRI or PET scans can exceed thousands of dollars. Families report difficult conversations with insurance companies denying tests as "experimental" while clinicians explain those tests could have shortened the diagnostic pathway by weeks or months. What many caregivers want to hear is not apology, but acknowledgment: this timeline is not normal, you are not impatient, and the uncertainty while waiting was a real cost, not just an inconvenience.

## Composite 3: Communication Gap with Clinicians About Daily-Life Impact

<!-- provenance: composite -->

In diagnostic appointments, the conversation often centers on test results, biomarker levels, and brain imaging findings. What gets less air time is what happens at home. A clinician might describe amyloid pathology or tau accumulation in clinical terms, and the family member in the room is translating silently: does this mean Mom can still manage her medications? Should we tell Dad he cannot drive? Will they know when they are lost? These are not small questions, and they do not appear on a neuropsychological exam score. Many caregivers report that they wanted to ask their doctor, "What does this diagnosis mean for their ability to do the things they need to do in daily life?"—but felt the appointment was not the place, or lacked the language to ask, or feared they were asking something already answered in medical terms they did not grasp. Some describe leaving the visit with a diagnosis but no clear sense of what support to set up, what to watch for, or what to plan. The gap is not always the clinician's fault; it is often the pace and format of the diagnostic visit, which prioritizes establishing the diagnosis over bridging it to the life the family actually lives.
