## What Dementia Is (and Is Not)

Dementia is not a single diagnosis but an umbrella term for a pattern of cognitive decline severe enough to interfere with daily life. [1][2] Memory loss is common, but dementia also affects language (struggling to find words or understand speech), spatial reasoning, decision-making, and social awareness. [3] Over 100 different brain diseases can cause dementia; the most common involve misfolded proteins (amyloid plaques and tau tangles) or vascular damage (strokes or reduced blood flow). [4] A memory lapse is not dementia. Normal aging means slowing down; dementia means losing abilities your parent once had—such as repeating questions, misplacing items regularly, or forgetting appointments despite reminders [21].

When coordinating with siblings or other family members, it helps to share this distinction early: confusion is not yet a diagnosis. This prevents different family members from drawing different conclusions about the same behavior and arriving at the appointment with conflicting concerns.

## The Most Common Types (in Plain Language)

**Alzheimer's disease** accounts for 60–80% of dementia cases. [5] It typically starts with memory problems, then spreads to language and reasoning. The brain accumulates two toxic proteins: amyloid plaques and tau tangles. Blood tests now detect these proteins before symptoms worsen.

**Vascular dementia** (15–20% of cases) results from strokes or reduced blood flow in the brain. Onset can be sudden or gradual. High blood pressure, diabetes, and prior strokes are common risk factors.

**Lewy body dementia** (10–15% of cases) features abnormal protein clumps in the brain. Hallucinations, stiff movements, and fluctuating alertness are hallmark signs. [6]

**Frontotemporal dementia** (5–10% of cases) damages the front and side lobes early, often causing personality changes before memory loss.

**Mixed dementia**—two or more diseases in the same brain—occurs in 20–30% of older adults at autopsy. [7] Most commonly, Alzheimer's combines with vascular damage or Lewy bodies.

When multiple adult children are involved, clarifying which type your parent has matters for dividing caregiving duties. Lewy body dementia, for example, often involves hallucinations or fluctuating alertness—information your siblings need to understand Mom's or Dad's good days and bad days.

> **Composite 1: Confusion Upon Hearing Diagnosis Terminology**
>
> When adult children first hear "mild cognitive impairment" or "probable Alzheimer's," reactions diverge. One sibling nods and accepts the label; another questions whether the testing was thorough enough; a third goes home and googles and feels immediately overwhelmed. Coordinating caregiving becomes harder when family members interpret the diagnosis differently. One wants Mom to stop driving immediately; another says she's fine. The label you receive in the appointment is the same, but the meanings attached differ across the family room.

## How a Diagnosis Is Actually Made

**The old way:** Cognitive testing and imaging ruled out other causes, but definitive diagnosis often came only at autopsy.

**The 2026 way:** Blood biomarkers now move first. [8] Here's the pathway:

1. **Cognitive and functional screening.** Your parent's doctor or neurologist asks about memory changes, how they affect daily life (managing money, medications, household tasks), and rules out depression, medications, or delirium. Bedside tests like the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA), Mini-Mental State Exam (MMSE) [22], or clock-drawing test take 15 minutes and detect whether thinking changes are significant enough to warrant further testing.

2. **Blood biomarkers (FDA-cleared, high accuracy).** [9] Three tests are now standard:
   - **pTau217** (Fujirebio Lumipulse, May 2025): >95% accuracy distinguishing Alzheimer's from other dementias. [10]
   - **pTau181** (Roche Elecsys, October 2025): Approved for primary care labs, detects amyloid and tau pathology. [11]
   - The ratio of phospho-tau to amyloid-beta in blood strongly predicts what PET imaging will show.

3. **Brain imaging (MRI, usually).** MRI rules out tumors, bleeding (subdural hematoma), or excess fluid (normal-pressure hydrocephalus). It also shows the regional pattern of brain shrinkage (atrophy) typical of each dementia type—such as memory-centers shrinking in Alzheimer's, stroke patterns in vascular dementia, or diffuse changes in Lewy body dementia [23].

4. **PET scan or cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) testing (only if needed).** If blood biomarkers and clinical picture are clear, these more invasive tests—such as amyloid PET scans and tau PET scans—can be skipped [24]. They're reserved for confusing cases where blood biomarker tests (pTau217 and pTau181) don't match the clinical presentation.

**Why blood biomarkers matter:** They have 91.7% concordance with PET imaging for positive results and 97.3% for negative results. [12] This means your parent's doctor can diagnose Alzheimer's disease from a single blood draw, not months of waiting and expensive imaging. Coordinate with co-caregivers to ensure the same person (or a designated lead) gathers all test results and shares them across the family.

> **Composite 2: Frustration with Diagnostic Workup Delays and Costs**
>
> The diagnostic pathway often stretches longer than families expect. Blood work, cognitive test, referral to neurologist, wait for appointment, imaging, possibly specialist review—each step takes weeks. Insurance may decline tests as "experimental." Meanwhile, siblings have different theories about what's wrong. By the time answers arrive, months have passed and family members feel they made decisions (about moving, stepping back from work, exploring care) on incomplete information. Designate one person as information hub and update siblings together when test results arrive.

## When the Answer Is We Are Not Sure

**Mild cognitive impairment (MCI):** Your parent has objective cognitive decline on testing or your report, but daily activities (paying bills and managing finances, taking medications on schedule, doing household shopping and cleaning) are still manageable. [13][14] The question is: will this progress to dementia?

Not automatically. 2–15% of people with MCI progress to dementia each year, depending on biomarker status. [15] Some stay stable for years; a few revert to normal cognition. If biomarkers are negative, prognosis is better. Early identification of amyloid and tau helps predict who will decline.

**Mixed pathology:** If your parent's imaging or biomarkers suggest multiple diseases (e.g., Alzheimer's pathology combined with vascular stroke patterns or Lewy bodies [25]), the doctor may defer the PET scan. Treatment approach is often similar regardless: slowing cognitive decline, managing vascular risk factors (blood pressure, cholesterol), addressing behavioral symptoms. [16]

**Normal cognition with biomarkers present:** A small number of cognitively normal older adults have amyloid or tau in their brains. This does not automatically mean dementia is coming. The doctor will monitor with repeat testing over years.

## What You Can Do Next

**If your parent has received a diagnosis:**

1. **Coordinate the family conversation.** Decide together: who attends the follow-up appointment? Who takes notes? Who will be the point person for answering questions from other siblings? Clarity reduces cross-family friction.

2. **Prepare shared questions.** Before the next appointment, ask co-caregivers: What do we most need to understand? What will this mean for Mom's driving, finances, living situation? Write these down together.

3. **Ask about treatment options.** If biomarkers confirm Alzheimer's disease, discuss anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies (lecanemab [17] or donanemab [18]) now approved for early-stage disease. These slow cognitive decline, not stop it; eligibility depends on biomarker status and cognitive stage. Share the explanation with siblings.

4. **Plan ahead together.** Early diagnosis enables advance directives, financial planning, family conversations, and access to clinical trials or therapies. [19] Divide tasks: one sibling researches treatment options, another helps with legal paperwork, etc.

> **Composite 3: Communication Gap with Clinicians About Daily-Life Impact**
>
> In diagnostic appointments, clinicians center on test results and imaging. What gets less air time is home life. Your parent's doctor describes amyloid pathology in clinical terms. You translate silently: does Dad still manage medications? Can he stay home alone? Should we tell him he can't drive? Many adult children report leaving the appointment with more questions than answers because the format prioritized diagnosis over what it means for the life your family actually lives. Bring co-caregivers; one can listen while the other takes notes or asks the follow-up: "What does this mean for whether Mom can live alone?"
