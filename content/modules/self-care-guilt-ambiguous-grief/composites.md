# Lived Experience Composites

## Relief and guilt in the same moment

Caregivers often describe a familiar sequence: they drop their family member off at adult day care or respite, get into the car alone, and feel physically lighter. The relief is immediate and involuntary. Then the guilt arrives just as quickly. The relief reads as evidence of not loving enough, not caring enough, not being the right person for the job. Many family members report the guilt lasting longer than the relief, sometimes hours after the respite period ends. The guilt does not match the situation—the person is safe, the caregiver needed the break, nothing went wrong—but it shows up anyway. Over time, some caregivers name this pattern out loud to a friend or support group. The naming does not make the guilt disappear, but it makes it less personal. They recognize it as a nearly universal response to long caregiving load, not as evidence that they are failing.

## The wish that this was over

Some caregivers catch themselves thinking about the end. Not planning it, not wanting to cause it, just noticing the thought: what would it be like if this was over? The thought is almost always followed by self-hatred. Wishing for an end feels like wishing someone dead, and that reads as unforgivable. Many family members report carrying this thought in silence for months or years, convinced they are the only one who has ever felt it. When they finally say it out loud—to a therapist, a support group, a trusted friend—they often learn that it is one of the most common thoughts in late-stage caregiving. The wish is not about cruelty or abandonment. It is about exhaustion, about watching someone suffer, about the doubleness of not wanting them to die and also not wanting this to continue. Professional caregivers who work in dementia care and palliative care name this as a normal part of anticipatory grief. It does not mean the caregiver loves less. It means the situation is unsustainable, and the caregiver's mind is trying to process that.

## Grieving someone still sitting in the room

Family members describe a specific kind of loss that lacks a name in everyday language: missing someone who is still physically present. The person is sitting at the table, but the partnership is gone. The parent is in the house, but the parent who used to guide them is not. The grief is real, but there is no funeral, no closure, no socially recognized space to mourn. Many caregivers report feeling disloyal for grieving someone who is still alive. They describe moments of acute awareness—a conversation that does not connect, a memory their loved one no longer holds, a familiar routine that now feels like caregiving rather than companionship—and the grief arrives in small, repeated doses. Some caregivers eventually recognize this as ambiguous loss, a term researchers use for grief without resolution. The grief does not mean they have given up. It means they are holding two truths at once: the person is still here, and they are also losing them. That doubleness does not resolve. Professional support, when caregivers seek it, often focuses not on closure but on holding both truths without requiring them to reconcile.
