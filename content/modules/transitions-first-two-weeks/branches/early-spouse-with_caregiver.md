## What this is

Your spouse has been diagnosed with dementia. You were in the room — or maybe you got the call afterward — and now you are home together in the house you share, and everything looks the same and nothing is the same. This module covers the first two weeks: what is normal to feel, what can wait, and the small number of things worth doing now.

<!-- provenance: composite -->
The particular cruelty of a spousal diagnosis is that it arrives inside a partnership. You did not sign up to be a caregiver. You signed up to be a husband, a wife, a partner. And the person sitting across from you at dinner tonight is still that person — still telling the same stories, still reaching for the remote, still asking what you want for breakfast. But somewhere between the parking lot and the front door, a line moved. You may already feel it: a quiet shift from "we decide together" to "I might need to decide for both of us." That shift is not something you asked for. Naming it is not disloyal. It is the truth of where you are standing.

How the diagnosis was made may look different from what you expected. The workup usually includes a history, a cognitive screen (often the MoCA, MMSE, or Mini-Cog), standard bloodwork, and imaging. In 2025–2026, many clinicians also use a blood biomarker test — measuring phosphorylated tau — as part of the workup [1][2][3]. As of 2025, two are FDA-cleared: the Fujirebio Lumipulse pTau217/Aβ42 ratio [2] and the Roche Elecsys pTau181 [3]. The Alzheimer's Association published its first clinical guideline on these tests in July 2025 [1]. Your doctor may have used one of these blood tests, a PET scan, a spinal tap, or a combination. The method does not change what to do this week.

## Why it matters now

The first two weeks are when spouses are most likely to swing between two extremes — taking over everything (finances, driving, medications, the yard) in a burst of protective energy, or pretending nothing has changed because the alternative is unbearable [5][7]. Neither one helps. The 2024 Lancet Commission found that timely support right after diagnosis reduces long-term caregiver distress [7]. This is not a crisis. It is a transition, and you and your spouse get to walk through it together — which is something adult children caring for a parent do not get in the same way.

<!-- provenance: composite -->
Spouses often describe a specific trap in the first week: the urge to quietly start doing things your partner used to handle — paying the bills, managing the medications, scheduling the appointments — without talking about it. It feels like protection. But what your spouse may experience is things disappearing from their life without explanation. And what you may experience, three weeks in, is resentment you did not see coming, because you took on work you were never asked to do. The first two weeks are not the time to redistribute every task. They are the time to notice the impulse and slow it down.

Most of what feels urgent right now does not have to be solved this week. Long-term financial planning, changing the will, deciding about the house, conversations with your children about "the future" — these do not get worse if you take ten days to breathe first. See the **Things That Can Wait** checklist.

## What to do this week

Pick one or two. Not all of them. The **First Two Weeks Action Items** checklist has the details.

**Write down what the clinician said** — together if your spouse is willing, alone if not. The type of dementia named (or "not yet specified"), medications started, tests pending, follow-up date (usually 2–4 weeks) [5]. You were both in the room. You may have heard different things. Writing it down while it is fresh helps.

**Have one honest conversation with your spouse about what they want.** Not a master plan. Just: "I heard what the doctor said. I want us to figure this out together. What matters to you right now?" Many spouses diagnosed with early-stage dementia are aware, frightened, and worried about being sidelined [7]. This conversation — before the extended family weighs in, before the internet advice starts — is between the two of you first.

**Tell one person outside the marriage.** A friend, a sibling, a neighbor. Not the whole family, not a group text [6]. One person who can hear it without panicking. If you need an opening: "I need to tell you something about [spouse's name]" or "We got some news, and I need to say it out loud." If there is no one safe, the Alzheimer's Association 24/7 Helpline (1-800-272-3900) is staffed by licensed clinicians [5].

<!-- provenance: composite -->
Spouses often resist telling anyone outside the marriage because it feels like a betrayal — like you are going behind your partner's back, or making it "real" in a way it was not before. Some describe the first phone call to a friend as harder than the diagnosis appointment itself. But the weight of this is too much for one marriage to hold without ventilation. Telling one person does not mean announcing it. It means letting one other human being know that your week was harder than it looked.

**Ask the clinic about the GUIDE program.** The CMS GUIDE Model (Guiding an Improved Dementia Experience) is live at 390 organizations as of July 2025 [4]. If your clinic participates, you may both be eligible for care navigation, caregiver education, and respite support — reimbursed up to roughly $2,500 per year (as of 2025) [4]. One phone call, or check cms.gov/guide.

**Locate key documents together.** Find the most recent driver's license or state ID for both of you, health insurance cards (including Medicare or Medicaid cards), both your powers of attorney, any existing healthcare proxy / advance directive. You probably know where these are — you may have filed them together. If they do not exist yet, that is a month-two task, not a week-one emergency [5].

**Do not rearrange the shared space yet.** The kitchen, the bathroom, the garage — leave them. Your spouse still lives here. Their competence is not gone. Safety-proofing can wait unless there is an immediate danger (stove left on, etc.) [5].

**Eat dinner together. Go to bed at the normal time.** The rhythms of your shared life are still intact. Protecting them this week is not denial. It is stability.

## When to call sooner

Some things in the first two weeks warrant a call back to the clinician — do not wait for the next appointment.

- **Sudden increased confusion over hours or days.** A noticeable jump in confusion can signal delirium — look for fever, sudden confusion markedly worse than baseline, or reduced alertness. A common trigger is a urinary tract infection [7]. Delirium is not the dementia getting worse overnight — it is a medical emergency layered on top, and it is treatable when caught early.
- **New medication side effects.** If a medication was started and your spouse develops nausea, dizziness, falls, or new agitation — call [5][6].
- **Driving feels unsafe right now.** If you are worried about your spouse behind the wheel today, call the clinic. They have a protocol for this [5]. This is not something you have to solve alone in the passenger seat.
- **Your spouse is expressing hopelessness or talking about not wanting to be here.** A dementia diagnosis can trigger depression. If your spouse is withdrawing sharply or expressing thoughts of self-harm — call [5][6]. This is not melodrama. It is a known risk after diagnosis.
- **Talk of self-harm or harm to others — from either of you.** Contact emergency services or call/text 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline). That is the system working as intended.

## How to care for yourself in this season

This is grief, and it is a kind almost no one outside this situation understands. Your spouse is right here — in the next room, beside you in bed, asking about lunch. And the future you planned together has changed shape. You are grieving a version of growing old that included two sharp minds. You are grieving the partnership as it was. And you are doing this while the person you are grieving for is still your person, still present, still themselves in a hundred ways that matter.

<!-- provenance: composite -->
What spouses rarely say out loud is the loneliness of lying next to someone who is changing. The person beside you falls asleep the same way. Their hand is the same hand. But something has shifted in the contract between you, and there is no one to talk to about it at 2 a.m. because the person you would normally talk to is the person this is about. That loneliness is not a failure of love. It is the specific shape of spousal grief, and it is different from what an adult child feels, different from what a friend feels. It deserves its own name and its own space.

The Lancet Commission found that even minimal early intervention — a single counseling session, a peer-support contact — reduced longer-term caregiver distress [7]. You do not need to find a support group tonight. But the Alzheimer's Association (1-800-272-3900) [5] and the National Institute on Aging's ADEAR Center (1-800-438-4380) [6] both have free caregiver resources, and there are groups specifically for spouses. When you are ready.

One thing that helps in the first two weeks, and it sounds absurd: do one thing that is just for you. Not for "us," not for your spouse's care. Walk around the block. Read a chapter. Sit in the car in the driveway for ten minutes with the radio on. The relationship you are in now requires you to remain a separate person with your own reserves. That is not selfish. It is structural.

Do not make any decisions you would not make on a regular Tuesday. The ground will steady, slowly, and when it does you will think more clearly than you can right now. The next module is there when you are ready, and not before.
