## What this is

Your parent has been diagnosed with dementia. They live with someone else — often your other parent, sometimes a sibling, sometimes another family member. You are not in that house. Someone else is.

This changes everything about how the first two weeks feel. You are carrying the diagnosis, but you are carrying it from outside the daily reality. The person inside the house — your other parent, your brother, your sister-in-law — is waking up next to it. They may have been the one who noticed the symptoms first. They may be the one who drove to the appointment. And right now, they are the one making breakfast while your parent asks the same question for the third time this morning.

<!-- provenance: composite -->
Adult children in this situation often describe a painful split: the urge to organize, coordinate, and fix from a distance, paired with the awareness that they are not the one doing the daily work. You may find yourself researching care options at midnight, drafting spreadsheets, sending articles to the family group text — and then feeling the silence when no one responds with the urgency you feel. Or you may feel paralyzed, unsure what your role is when someone else is already there. Both positions are disorienting, and neither one is wrong.

How the diagnosis was reached may look different from what you expected. The workup usually includes a history, a cognitive screen (often the MoCA, MMSE, or Mini-Cog), standard bloodwork, and imaging. In 2025–2026, many clinicians also use a blood biomarker test — measuring phosphorylated tau — as part of the diagnostic workup [1][2][3]. As of 2025, two are FDA-cleared: the Fujirebio Lumipulse pTau217/Aβ42 ratio [2] and the Roche Elecsys pTau181 [3]. The Alzheimer's Association published its first clinical guideline on these tests in July 2025, reporting agreement with PET brain scans 91.7% of the time when positive and 97.3% when negative [1]. Your parent's doctor may have used a blood test, a PET scan, a spinal tap, or some combination. The method does not change what to do this week.

## Why it matters now

The most common mistake in the first two weeks, when your parent lives with other family, is not about doing too much or too little. It is about the gap between the person inside the house and the person outside it.

The person inside the house is absorbing the diagnosis through daily life — through the look on your parent's face at dinner, through the repeated question, through the quiet. They may not have the bandwidth to research, plan, or even return your calls promptly. They are running on proximity and adrenaline.

The person outside the house — you — is absorbing it through information and imagination. You are reading, planning, worrying. You may have more time to think, and thinking can feel like helping. But the in-house person may experience your research and suggestions as pressure, or as criticism of what they are already doing.

The 2024 Lancet Commission found that timely support after diagnosis reduces long-term caregiver distress — and the data is clear that the in-home caregiver's distress in the first months predicts their trajectory for years [7]. The person living with your parent is the one most at risk of burning out. Your most important job in the first two weeks is not to solve the problem. It is to make sure the person in the house does not feel alone in it.

<!-- provenance: composite -->
When the in-house person is your other parent — a well spouse caring for a spouse with dementia — the dynamic is particularly delicate. They are losing a partner in slow motion. They may minimize what is happening, because admitting it means admitting the marriage has changed. Or they may be terrified and not saying so, because they have been the strong one for fifty years. Telling a 78-year-old mother that she "needs to accept help" in the first week rarely lands. Asking her "What was the hardest part of today?" sometimes does.

The things that genuinely can wait — long-term financial restructuring, choosing a facility, telling every cousin, changing the will — are listed in the **Things That Can Wait** checklist. And this includes the big family coordination meeting. You do not need to have "the family talk" this week. You need to get through this week.

## What to do this week

Your role this week is not to run the household from outside. It is to support the person who is inside it, and to gather the information that will matter in month two. Pick one or two of these. The **First Two Weeks Action Items** checklist has the full details.

**Write down what the clinician said.** If you were at the appointment, do this now: type of dementia named (or "not yet specified"), medications started, tests pending, follow-up date (usually 2–4 weeks) [5]. If you were not at the appointment, ask the person who was — gently. They may not have retained the details. If nobody wrote anything down, call the clinic. You are allowed to ask. Twice, if needed.

**Talk to the person in the house — and lead with their experience, not your plan.** This is the most important action item on this list, and it is not about logistics. Call them. Do not open with "So here's what I've been reading" or "I made a list of things we should do." Open with: "How are you? What has this week been like?" [6]. Listen to the whole answer. The logistics will follow — but if the in-house person feels managed instead of supported, the coordination you need from them will not happen.

<!-- provenance: composite -->
Adult children who try to organize care from outside often describe hitting a wall they did not expect: the in-house person does not want to be organized. Your mother does not want a spreadsheet. Your brother does not want a shared calendar. This is not stubbornness — it is the difference between someone living inside a situation and someone analyzing it from outside. The rhythm of the household was not designed for your workflow. The first two weeks are too early to impose a system. They are not too early to ask: "What would actually help you this week?" and mean it.

**Ask the clinic if they participate in the GUIDE program.** The CMS GUIDE Model (Guiding an Improved Dementia Experience) went live at 390 organizations in July 2025 [4]. If your parent's clinic participates, the household may be eligible for care navigation, caregiver education, and respite support — reimbursed up to roughly $2,500 per year (as of 2025) [4]. The in-house caregiver is the person who will benefit most from care navigation. You can make the call — check at cms.gov/guide, or phone the clinic directly. It is one concrete thing you can do from outside.

**Locate key documents — or help the in-house person find them.** Driver's license or state ID, health insurance cards (including Medicare or Medicaid cards), power of attorney, healthcare proxy / advance directive. If these are in your parent's house, the in-house person likely knows where they are. Ask without implying they should have already found them. If the documents do not exist, that is a month-two task [5].

**Assess — do not assume — the in-house person's capacity.** Your 80-year-old mother may be more capable than you think. Your brother may be less available than he says. The in-house caregiver's actual bandwidth — their own health, their work schedule, their emotional state — determines what is realistic. Do not plan around the version of them you wish were there. Plan around the version who is [7].

**Know that treatment options may come up.** Your clinician may mention disease-modifying therapies that did not exist a few years ago [7]. That is a month-one conversation between the clinical team, your parent, and the family — not a week-one decision. If you are not the one at the appointments, ask the in-house person to take notes or to call you from the waiting room afterward.

**Eat something. Sleep if you can.** This applies to you and to the person in the house [6].

## When to call sooner

You are relying on the in-house person to notice changes — and they may not always know what counts as a "call the doctor" moment. One useful thing you can do early: share this short list with them.

- **Sudden increased confusion over hours or days.** A noticeable jump in confusion — not the gradual kind, but a change from yesterday — can signal delirium. Look for fever, confusion markedly worse than baseline, or reduced alertness; a common trigger is a urinary tract infection [7]. Delirium is not the dementia getting worse overnight. It is a medical emergency layered on top, and it is treatable when caught early. The in-house person may describe this as "something is really off today." That description is enough to call.
- **New medication side effects.** If a medication was started and your parent develops nausea, dizziness, falls, or new agitation — the in-house person should call the clinic [5][6]. If they hesitate ("I don't want to bother the doctor"), give them permission: the doctor expects this call.
- **Safety concerns.** If the in-house person mentions the stove being left on, your parent wandering at night, or confusion about where they are — call the clinic together if possible [5].
- **The in-house person sounds like they are breaking.** This is not on the standard call-the-doctor list, but it belongs here. If your other parent sounds exhausted, tearful, or like they are minimizing something serious — that is a signal. Not necessarily to call the clinician, but to get yourself or another family member physically there, or to arrange outside help this week. The in-house person burning out in the first month is one of the most predictable and preventable crises in dementia care [7].
- **Talk of self-harm or harm to others — by your parent or the in-house caregiver.** Call the clinic or emergency services. That is the system working as intended [5][6].

If **you** are the one not okay — if the helplessness of being outside the situation is more than you can carry, if you are having thoughts of hurting yourself — contact emergency services or call/text 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline).

## How to care for yourself in this season

Your grief has a specific shape: you are losing a parent, and you are watching someone else do the daily work of that loss. The in-house person is there for the hard morning. You are there for the phone call about the hard morning. Both positions are painful, and they are painful in different ways.

<!-- provenance: composite -->
What is rarely said out loud is that outside caregivers often feel like they have no right to be as upset as they are. You are not the one getting up at 3 a.m. You are not the one managing the meals. Your grief can feel secondhand, borrowed, unjustified. But the grief of watching your parent's diagnosis unfold through someone else's reports — piecing together the picture from phone calls and texts, imagining what the house sounds like at night — is its own kind of loss. And the helplessness of wanting to do more from where you are, and not being able to, is a weight that compounds quietly.

The Lancet Commission found that even minimal early intervention — a single counseling session, a peer-support contact, a structured information packet — reduced longer-term caregiver distress [7]. This applies to you, not just to the person in the house. You do not need to join a support group this week. But the Alzheimer's Association (1-800-272-3900) [5] and the National Institute on Aging's ADEAR Center (1-800-438-4380) [6] both have free resources for family caregivers — including those coordinating from a distance.

What helps in the first two weeks, when you are the outside person, is to name what you can actually do — and to stop punishing yourself for what you cannot. You can make phone calls. You can research. You can listen. You can show up on a weekend. You can send a meal. You cannot be in two places. You cannot make the in-house person accept help they are not ready for. You cannot undo the diagnosis. The things you can do are smaller than the things you wish you could do, and that gap is where the guilt lives. The guilt is not useful. Set it down when you can.

Do not make any decisions about your own life — moving, changing jobs (check FMLA eligibility before quitting [8]), upending your household — that you would not make on a regular Tuesday. And do not make decisions about your parent's household without the in-house person's buy-in. The coordination will come. It does not have to come this week. The next module is there when you are ready.
