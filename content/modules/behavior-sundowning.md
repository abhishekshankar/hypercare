## What you might see

Sundowning is not a single symptom. It is a pattern: your family member may seem more confused, worried, or irritable in the late afternoon and evening than they did in the morning. You might see pacing, shadowing (following you from room to room), repeated questions, or trouble sitting still. Light levels change, everyone is more tired, and the brain is working hard to make sense of the day. You are not imagining it, and you did not cause it.

## What often plays a role

A few things tend to show up in the same window of time. Fatigue builds by evening. Measured in plain language, the brain is simply less resourced to handle noise, too many people talking at once, or a hungry stomach. Discomfort (pain, thirst, or needing the bathroom) can look like agitation. Low light and long shadows can make rooms feel unfamiliar. These factors stack; small fixes sometimes help a lot, even when they do not remove every behavior.

## What you can try the same day

Start with the basics, one at a time, so you can notice what actually helps. Offer a small snack and water before your family member is too tired to eat. Turn on more lamps before the room goes dim; add a nightlight in hallways. Lower competing noise: one conversation, calmer music or silence. If they pace, a short walk in a safe, familiar place can use energy productively. When they follow you, give them a small job (holding a towel, setting napkins) that keeps them near you without you feeling trapped. Name one feeling out loud, slowly: “It sounds like you are worried. I am right here.” You are not looking for a perfect evening; you are looking for a little more ease.

## When to get medical input

Sundowning overlaps with pain, infection, vision changes, sleep disorders, and medication side effects. If the change is new, much worse, or comes with fever, new stiffness, a fall, or trouble swallowing, call your clinician. Same day, call if the person cannot be redirected from unsafe behavior, or you feel unsafe. You deserve guidance when patterns shift, not only in a crisis.

## How to care for yourself in this season

This rhythm is hard on you. The hours when you are most tired are the hours when they need more presence. That conflict is real. Shrink the plan to what one evening can hold. Trade off with another person when you can, even for an hour, so your nervous system can come down. What you are doing is skilled work, not a character test you pass or fail.
