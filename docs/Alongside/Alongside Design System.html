A design system for Alongside

For the person reading this at 3 a.m., in a dark hallway, between hard moments.

Alongside is used by family caregivers of people with dementia — often exhausted, often older, almost always on a phone, frequently at night. This document specifies the foundational visual and interaction language of the product. It is not a marketing system. It is not a consumer SaaS system. It is a quiet, editorial system: closer to a serious publication or a thoughtful nonprofit than to a productivity app.

Principle Every choice here — the type, the color, the radii, the restraint — serves one question: will this feel steady to someone who is not steady right now? When in doubt, we choose calm over clever, and legibility over personality.

Design principles

  1. Calm, not cheerful. No confetti, no streaks, no "You did it!" The reward for using Alongside is clarity, not dopamine.

  2. Readable first, beautiful second. Type sizes, contrast, and line length are chosen for a tired 65-year-old at 11 p.m., not a designer at 2 p.m.

  3. Editorial over interface. Where we can use words, hierarchy, and whitespace, we do. Chrome is minimal; content is primary.

  4. One warm voice. A single warm primary (moss), a single urgency accent (amber). We resist adding a third color until the product earns it.

  5. Motion you do not notice. Transitions under 200ms, no parallax, no bounce. If you see the animation, we failed.

01 — Typography

A quiet serif for the human voice. A sans for everything else.

Caregivers often read Alongside late at night on a phone they are half-holding. The type system optimizes for that reader: large minimum body size, generous line height, short line length, and a pairing that feels editorial rather than clinical.

Typeface rationale

Primary — Source Serif 4 (display & long-form) A contemporary text serif with open counters, a calm vertical axis, and a true italic (not a slant). It reads as considered and human — closer to a hospice pamphlet or a NYT Well column than to a technology brand. It replaces Georgia's warmth with better on-screen hinting, and avoids the fashion-magazine theatrics of Playfair or Didone serifs.
Secondary — Inter (UI, data, labels) A neutral, workmanlike grotesque with excellent screen legibility at small sizes. It carries no personality — which is what we want from UI. Inter handles the labels, form fields, buttons, and data; the serif carries voice. The pairing reads editorial rather than app-like.
Fallback stack Georgia, 'Times New Roman', serif for the serif; -apple-system, 'Segoe UI', sans-serif for the sans. Both are chosen so that when webfonts fail to load (common on older phones with poor connectivity), the fallback still reads correctly.

Type scale

All sizes are in px at a base of 17px (not 16). The base was raised deliberately — see rationale below.

Why 17px base, not 16 Our target reader is often 55–75 with presbyopic vision and is reading on a phone at arm's length in dim light. 17px body, 1.6 line-height, ~62ch line length lifts the baseline reading experience out of "technically legible" and into "comfortable." On iOS, 17pt is already Apple's default body size; on Android we simply follow suit.

Usage rules

  • Italic is voice. Italics are reserved for the person speaking — a headline that sounds like a human saying something gentle. Never for emphasis inside body text; use sans + weight instead.

  • Line length. Target 55–72 characters per line on any reading surface. Set max-width: 62ch on body containers.

  • No ALL CAPS in body. Reserved for the caption role. Uppercase body text is harder to read, which matters here.

  • Numbers use tabular figures in any UI context (font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums), so counts and timestamps don't shift.

02 — Color

Warm paper. Moss. One flare of amber for when it matters.

The palette is intentionally narrow. Neutrals are warm (never blue-white); the primary is a moss green that reads grounded rather than corporate; the urgency accent is burnt amber, not red — closer to a lamp in a dark room than an alarm. Semantic colors are muted, not saturated.

Why warm neutrals, not white Pure white #FFFFFF on a phone at night is physically uncomfortable and emotionally cold. Our paper (#F7F4EE) is a soft, recycled-paper cream; it reads serious (like a printed program, not a kids' app) and is kinder to tired eyes. The dark mode equivalent (#141413) is not pure black either — it is a warm near-black that reduces halation around text on OLED screens.
Why moss, not forest or teal Moss (#415A3E) is a low-chroma green with a brown undertone. It does not carry "success/healthy" signaling (that belongs to a separate semantic green). It is grounded and outdoorsy without feeling outdoorsy-branded. It passes AA contrast on our paper at all body sizes.
Why burnt amber for urgency, not red Red, in this product, would be cruel. A caregiver opening the app in the middle of a crisis does not need their screen to yell at them. Burnt amber (#B8531A) communicates "this matters, attend to this" without the clinical-emergency register of red. Red is reserved strictly for the error semantic state (failed form submission, etc.) — not for the crisis strip.

03 — Spacing & Radius

Eight-point grid. Four radii. No exceptions.

One grid, a very small radius set, no decorative spacing values. Restraint here is load-bearing: it makes the product feel composed rather than assembled.

Spacing scale (8pt grid)

Multiples of 8, with 4 as a half-step for dense in-component padding (e.g. chip internal padding). No values outside the scale.

Component conventions

  • Tap target minimum: 44×44px for any interactive element. Most land at 48px.

  • Content padding on mobile: 20px horizontal, 24px vertical (s-5 both axes is acceptable).

  • Card internal padding: 20px (s-5).

  • Section separation: 48–64px between major blocks on mobile; 64–96px on desktop.

Radius scale

Why small radii Large radii (16px+) and pills everywhere read as playful/consumer. We use them sparingly — pills only for buttons and chips, where they help with tap affordance. Everything else uses 4–12px. This keeps the surface feel editorial rather than app-like.

04 — Elevation

Three shadows. All barely visible.

Elevation is used for affordance, not decoration. We do not have colored glows, large drop shadows, or floating pill chrome. Shadows are warm-tinted (slight ink warmth, not neutral gray) to match the paper.

elev-1
Resting surface. Cards at rest.
elev-2
Hover, popover, small sheet.
elev-3
Modal, bottom sheet.
Rule of thumb If you need a shadow stronger than elev-3, you need a modal overlay instead. We do not stack elevations.

06 — Accessibility

Baked in. Not a pass at the end.

Our readers are disproportionately older, tired, and one-handed. Accessibility is a feature here, not a compliance checkbox.

Minimum contrast

  • Body text, help text, anything informational: AAA (7:1) minimum. If it can't clear AAA, it's not body text — it's caption, and it can't be load-bearing.

  • UI controls, buttons, icons: AA (4.5:1) minimum against their background, including hover and disabled states.

  • Decorative borders & rules: No contrast minimum, but a second non-color affordance (shape, text, position) must always exist.

Tap targets

Every interactive element has a minimum 44 × 44pt hit area (iOS HIG floor). Most components in this system default to 48pt. Inline text links inside body copy are the only exception and must be accompanied by sufficient line-height (1.5+) to keep adjacent targets separable.

Focus ring

Spec: 2px solid focus, 3px offset from the element. Applied via :focus-visible only — never on mouse click. Always visible over any background (tested on paper, paper-3, moss, amber, error, and modal backdrops). Never removed without replacement.

Motion reduction

The system respects prefers-reduced-motion: reduce globally.

  • Transitions longer than 10ms are clamped to 10ms.

  • Modal and sheet entrances swap from fade to instant-show.

  • Any decorative motion (skeleton shimmer, subtle pulses) is disabled.

  • The only motion that remains is directly caused by user input — e.g. a scroll, a sheet the user is dragging. Implicit motion is removed.

Language & screen readers

  • All interactive elements have an accessible name (aria-label or visible text).

  • Icons are aria-hidden when adjacent to a text label; stand-alone icon buttons carry their own label.

  • Status regions (save confirmations, errors) use aria-live="polite"; the crisis strip is never live-announced on every page — it is a persistent landmark.

  • Lang is set per document; date/time formats come from the user's locale, not a hard-coded format.

Zoom & reflow

All layouts reflow cleanly at 200% browser zoom without horizontal scrolling (WCAG 1.4.10). Line length is enforced via ch-based max-width rather than px, so text stays readable as the user scales up.

07 — What we don't do

Three deliberate absences.

A design system is as much about what it refuses as what it includes. These three refusals are load-bearing.

No gradients

Gradients — even subtle ones — read as marketing. They are the visual language of landing pages, of "sign up for our newsletter," of AI apps. Our surfaces are single flat tones of warm paper. The only place color transitions are allowed is inside photographs or illustrations chosen for editorial purpose. Backgrounds, buttons, banners, cards, and headings are all flat fills.

No emoji in UI

Emoji carry tone we cannot control. An emoji ✨ next to "Great job!" may feel fine to a designer and devastating to a user whose mother just didn't recognize them. Our affordances are words and shape. Status indicators use color + text (never color alone, never color + emoji). If we need a glyph, we use a stroked Lucide icon at 1.5 stroke weight, semantically labeled.

No attention-seeking motion

No bounce, no spring, no parallax, no confetti, no pulse to draw the eye, no floating elements, no page-transition shimmer, no animated illustrations. A caregiver's attention is already spent elsewhere. Motion in this product serves function only — a sheet opening so the user understands where they came from, a form field settling into focus. If you can notice the animation, it is too much.

The underlying test For any visual or motion choice, ask: would this feel okay to someone whose day is already hard? If the answer isn't an unambiguous yes, remove it. Alongside's job is to get out of the way.
